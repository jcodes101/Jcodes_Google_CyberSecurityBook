# Threat actors (hackers, APTs, and access points)

A **threat actor** is any person or group that presents a security risk. This includes people **inside** and **outside** an organization, and it includes both intentional threats and accidental risks.

---

## Threat actor categories (by motivation)

Threat actors are commonly grouped into five categories:

- **Competitors**: rival companies that might benefit from leaked information
- **State actors**: government intelligence agencies
- **Criminal syndicates**: organized groups that profit from criminal activity
- **Insider threats**: people with (or who previously had) authorized access—can be accidental or intentional
- **Shadow IT**: people using technologies that lack IT governance (e.g., using personal email for work)

---

## Hackers (and intent)

Threat actors may gain unauthorized access by hacking. A **hacker** is someone who uses computers to gain unauthorized access to systems, networks, or data. Like “threat actor,” “hacker” is a broad term and does not automatically describe intent.

In security, hackers are often described by intent:

### Unauthorized (unethical / malicious) hackers

Individuals who use their skills to commit crimes. Skill level varies widely. Some have limited skills and rely on pre-written tools—often called **script kiddies**.

### Authorized (ethical) hackers

People who use hacking skills to improve security (internal security teams, external vendors, or freelancers). Some organizations incentivize reporting through **bug bounty** programs.

### Semi-authorized hackers

Individuals who may violate ethical standards but are not considered malicious. Example: **hacktivists** may exploit vulnerabilities to advance a political goal or raise awareness of risks.

---

## Advanced persistent threats (APTs)

An **advanced persistent threat (APT)** is when a threat actor maintains unauthorized access to a system for an extended period of time.

APTs are commonly associated with nation states and state-sponsored actors and often focus on surveillance and intelligence gathering. That intelligence can be used to manipulate services such as government, defense, financial, and telecom systems.

Even if APTs are often linked to state actors, private businesses can still be targeted—sometimes as stepping stones to larger entities. APTs tend to be stealthy because high-value intrusions are costly and time-consuming.

---

## Access points (attack vector categories)

Threat actors can gain access through many vectors. Common categories include:

- **Direct access**: physical access to a system
- **Removable media**: portable hardware (e.g., USB drives)
- **Social media**: communication/content-sharing platforms
- **Email**: personal or business accounts
- **Wireless networks**: on-premises wireless access
- **Cloud services**: third-party hosted services
- **Supply chains**: third-party vendors that can become backdoors into systems

Recognizing a threat actor’s motivations can help you anticipate which access points they might target. For example, remote workers are more likely to present email-related risk than direct physical access risk.

---

## Key takeaways

- Defending an attack surface starts with thinking like a threat actor.
- Not all threat actors intend harm—some risks are accidental.
- Understanding motivations and matching them to likely attack vectors is a valuable attacker-mindset skill.
