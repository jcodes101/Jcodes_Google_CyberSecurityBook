# The data lifecycle and data governance

The **data lifecycle** is an important model security teams consider when protecting information. It influences how policies are set to align with business objectives and helps guide the technologies used to keep information accessible.

---

## The data lifecycle (5 stages)

In general, the data lifecycle has five stages that describe how data flows through an organization from creation until it is no longer useful:

- **Collect**
- **Store**
- **Use**
- **Archive**
- **Destroy**

Protecting information at each stage highlights the need to keep data **accessible** and **recoverable** if something goes wrong.

---

## Data governance

Businesses handle massive amounts of data every day, collecting new information from internal and external sources. A structured approach helps keep data private and secure.

**Data governance** is a set of processes that define how an organization manages information. Governance often includes policies that specify how to keep data **private**, **accurate**, **available**, and **secure** throughout its lifecycle.

Data governance is collaborative and relies on people. Policies commonly define roles:

- **Data owner**: decides who can access, edit, use, or destroy their information
- **Data custodian**: responsible for the safe handling, transport, and storage of information
- **Data steward**: maintains and implements data governance policies set by an organization

Because organizations store, move, and transform data across many IT systems, governance policies assign accountability to owners, custodians, and stewards.

**Note:** As a data custodian, you will primarily be responsible for maintaining security and privacy rules for your organization.

---

## Protecting data at every stage

Most security plans include a policy that outlines how information will be managed across an organization—often called a **data governance policy**.

These documents:

- define procedures to keep data safe
- limit who or what can access data
- clarify expectations for participating in data protection

Security professionals play an important role in data governance. As a **data custodian**, you help ensure data isn’t damaged, stolen, or misused.

---

## Legally protected information

Data represents real people and their lives (purchases, decisions, health information, and more). Because of this, data owners should decide whether to share their data, and their privacy decisions must be respected.

Securing data can be challenging because data owners may generate more data than they can manage, which can leave custodians and stewards without explicit instructions for every situation. Governments and regulators help by defining categories of information that must be protected by default:

- **PII (personally identifiable information)**: information used to infer an individual’s identity; can be used to contact or locate someone
- **PHI (protected health information)**:
  - **U.S.**: regulated by **HIPAA**; defined as “information that relates to the past, present, or future physical or mental health or condition of an individual”
  - **EU**: similar concept regulated by the **GDPR**
- **SPII (sensitive personally identifiable information)**: a type of PII under stricter handling guidelines; should be accessed only on a need-to-know basis (e.g., bank account numbers, login credentials)

Overall, personal information should be protected from unauthorized use and disclosure.

---

## Key takeaways

Many organizations use data governance policies to plan how they will protect sensitive information.

- The data lifecycle has five stages: **collect, store, use, archive, destroy**
- Data governance defines processes and roles for managing information
- As a **data custodian**, you help keep information accessible and safe throughout its lifecycle
- Important protected-data categories include **PII**, **PHI**, and **SPII**
