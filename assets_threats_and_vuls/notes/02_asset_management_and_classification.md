# Asset management and asset classification

**Asset management** is the process of tracking assets and the risks that affect them. The idea is simple: you can only protect what you know you have.

Previously, you learned that identifying, tracking, and classifying assets are important parts of asset management. This note focuses on the purpose and benefits of **asset classification** and common classification levels.

---

## Why asset management matters

Keeping assets safe requires a workable system that helps businesses operate smoothly. Building and maintaining that system requires detailed knowledge of assets in an environment.

Example: A bank needs money available each day to serve customers. Equipment, devices, and processes must ensure money is both **available** and **secure** from unauthorized access.

Organizations protect many types of assets, including:

- **Digital assets** (e.g., customer data, financial records)
- **Information systems** that process data (e.g., networks, software)
- **Physical assets** (e.g., facilities, equipment, supplies)
- **Intangible assets** (e.g., brand reputation, intellectual property)

Regardless of type, assets should be classified and accounted for.

---

## Asset classification (what it is)

**Asset classification** is the practice of labeling assets based on their **sensitivity** and **importance** to an organization.

Determining sensitivity and importance varies by organization, but typically requires knowing:

- what you have
- where it is
- who owns it
- how important it is

Classifying assets based on these characteristics helps determine their sensitivity and value.

---

## Common asset classifications

Asset classification supports effective risk management by helping organizations:

- prioritize security resources
- reduce IT costs
- stay compliant with legal regulations

A common classification scheme is:

- **Restricted** (highest): extremely sensitive, need-to-know information
- **Confidential**: disclosure may cause significant negative impact
- **Internal-only**: available to employees and business partners
- **Public** (lowest): no negative consequence if released

How the scheme is applied depends on the characteristics of the asset. Identifying an asset’s **owner** is sometimes the most complicated characteristic to determine.

**Note:** Some organizations vary at the highest levels. For example, government organizations may label their most sensitive assets as **confidential** instead of **restricted**.

---

## Challenges of classifying information

Some assets have obvious owners (e.g., a building). Information is often harder to classify.

Example: A business issues a laptop to an employee. The business might be the owner, but the employee may store personal data (like photos) on the device, complicating ownership and classification.

Another challenge: information can have **multiple classification values** at the same time.

Example: A letter addressed to you contains public information (your name) and confidential information (your address) together.

---

## Key takeaways

Every business has different requirements for its security strategy. Understanding why and how organizations classify assets is important for security professionals.

Information is one of the most important assets, and cybersecurity professionals help protect it from damage, disclosure, and misuse. Recognizing the challenges in classifying information helps organizations meet their security needs.
