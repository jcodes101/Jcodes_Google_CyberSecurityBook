# Being prepared for anything (attacker mindset + simulations)

Having a plan for when things go wrong is important—but first you need to decide what to plan for. Security teams often run simulations of things that can go wrong as part of vulnerability management by applying an **attacker mindset** to weaknesses they discover.

Adopting an attacker mindset is like running an experiment: cause problems in a controlled environment, evaluate outcomes, and use insights to build or improve security plans.

---

## Simulating threats (proactive vs reactive)

Attack simulations are typically done in two ways, both intended to make systems safer:

- **Proactive simulations**: assume the role of an attacker by exploiting vulnerabilities and breaking through defenses (often called a **red team** exercise).
- **Reactive simulations**: assume the role of a defender responding to an attack (often called a **blue team** exercise).

Both are team efforts, and analysts can be involved in either.

### Proactive simulations (red team)

Proactive teams often spend more time planning than executing. They may use a range of tactics, such as testing security awareness by sending fictitious emails to see whether staff can be persuaded to disclose login credentials.

### Reactive simulations (blue team)

Reactive teams focus on understanding the assets they protect and identifying weaknesses—often with the help of **vulnerability scanning** tools.

---

## Scanning for trouble (vulnerability scanners in reactive work)

A **vulnerability scanner** automatically compares known vulnerabilities and exposures against the technologies on a network. These tools are commonly used in the field.

Reactive simulations often use scan results to weigh risk and decide how to remediate issues.

Example workflow aligned with vulnerability assessment steps:

- **Identification**: a vulnerable server is flagged (e.g., running an outdated OS).
- **Vulnerability analysis**: research the OS and related vulnerabilities.
- **Risk assessment**: score severity and evaluate the impact of not fixing issues.
- **Remediation**: use findings to address the problem (patching, configuration changes, etc.).

---

## Reporting and communication

Exercises like these typically produce a report of findings. Results may be shared with service providers or supervisors. Clearly communicating outcomes is an important skill for security professionals.

---

## Finding innovative solutions

Many security controls were created reactively because criminals continually look for ways to bypass defenses. Applying an attacker mindset effectively requires staying current on security trends and emerging technologies.

**Pro tip:** Resources like the **NIST National Vulnerability Database (NVD)** can help you stay current on common vulnerabilities.

---

## Key takeaways

- Vulnerability assessments support security risk planning.
- Analysts may participate in proactive (red team) and reactive (blue team) simulations.
- Scans and structured assessment steps help turn findings into remediation actions.
- Staying informed about both vulnerabilities and emerging technologies supports an innovative security mindset.
