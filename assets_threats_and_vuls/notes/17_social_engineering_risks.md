# Social engineering risks

**Social engineering** is deception that takes advantage of how people think. It preys on feelings like curiosity, generosity, and excitement. Threat actors use those feelings to influence judgment and bypass technical defenses, which is why social engineering can be extremely harmful (and often easy to carry out).

---

## Example: Twitter hack (2020)

One high-profile example is the **Twitter hack of 2020**. Hackers called Twitter employees pretending to be IT. That impersonation helped them gain access to internal tools and take over high-profile accounts (politicians, celebrities, entrepreneurs).

This highlights why defenses must combine technology controls with user awareness.

---

## Signs and common social engineering techniques

Social engineering is dangerous because people often don’t realize they’re being targeted until it’s too late.

Common techniques include:

- **Baiting**: tempts someone into compromising security (e.g., **USB baiting** with an infected USB drive).
- **Phishing**: digital communications that trick people into revealing sensitive data or deploying malicious software (commonly via email).
- **Quid pro quo**: promises a reward in exchange for access, information, or money (e.g., impersonating a loan officer offering a “better rate” if the victim shares account details).
- **Tailgating (piggybacking)**: an unauthorized person follows an authorized person into a restricted area.
- **Watering hole**: compromising a website frequently visited by a target group; those sites may deliver malware (example referenced: **Holy Water attack (2020)** affecting religious/charity/volunteer websites).

These techniques can target anyone—from entry-level staff to executives.

---

## Encouraging caution (security awareness focus areas)

Comprehensive security training helps reduce risk. For social engineering, focus on:

- **Stay alert** for suspicious communications and unknown people (especially email). Watch for spelling errors and verify the sender name and address.
- **Be cautious sharing information**, especially on social media (attackers mine profiles for details they can weaponize).
- **Control curiosity** when something seems too good to be true (avoid clicking unknown attachments/links).

**Pro tip:** Layer technical defenses (e.g., firewalls, **MFA**, block lists, email filtering) so mistakes don’t immediately become incidents.

Training should extend beyond employees when possible—educating customers also helps reduce social engineering success.

Security analysts support this by testing systems, documenting best practices, and promoting safe behaviors across the organization.

---

## Key takeaways

- Social engineering works because people want to help and tend to trust others.
- It often bypasses technical defenses by manipulating human decision-making.
- Recognizing common techniques and implementing layered controls + training significantly reduces risk.
