# The origins of phishing (and how it evolved)

Phishing has existed since the early days of the internet and can be traced back to the **1990s**. As more people came online, malicious actors recognized that the internet offered anonymity that could be used to commit crimes.

---

## Early persuasion tactics (1990s)

One early phishing wave targeted **AOL Instant Messenger (AIM)**. Users received messages asking them to verify accounts or provide billing details. The messages were sent by malicious actors impersonating service providers.

This became an early example of **mass phishing**: sending malicious messages to a large number of people to increase the chance that someone will fall for it.

During the AIM attacks, emails were crafted to look like they came from AOL, using official-looking logos, colors, and fonts. Stolen information was then used to create fraudulent AOL accounts that enabled other anonymous criminal activity.

AOL responded by adapting security policies and warning users on-platform about phishing.

---

## How phishing evolved (early 2000s)

Phishing continued evolving as businesses and new technologies expanded online. In the early 2000s, **e-commerce** and **online payments** grew rapidly, creating new opportunities for criminals.

Email phishing remained common and expanded from stealing credentials/payment data to distributing malicious software.

In late 2003, attackers created fraudulent websites resembling services like **eBay** and **PayPal™**, and mass campaigns also targeted e-commerce and banking sites to distribute malicious programs.

---

## Common phishing types to know

Five common types of phishing every security analyst should recognize:

- **Email phishing**: messages sent via email pretending to be a trusted person/entity.
- **Smishing**: phishing via text messaging (SMS) and other phone messaging services (e.g., iMessage, WhatsApp).
- **Vishing**: voice calls or voice messages that trick targets into sharing sensitive information.
- **Spear phishing**: targeted email phishing aimed at specific people (e.g., a company’s accountants).
- **Whaling**: a form of spear phishing targeting high-ranking executives.

---

## Recent trends (2010s and targeted phishing)

Starting in the 2010s, criminals increasingly shifted from broad mass phishing toward **targeted phishing**—highly customized attacks designed to feel familiar to a specific target.

One targeted technique is **angler phishing**:

- attackers impersonate customer service representatives on social media
- they watch for public complaints (message boards, comments) and intercept them
- they contact frustrated customers using accounts that resemble real businesses
- they trick targets into sharing sensitive information by promising to fix the problem

This mirrors the AIM-era strategy: believable impersonation + social pressure to act quickly.

---

## Key takeaways

- Phishing tactics have become more sophisticated, and older methods (especially email phishing) are still effective.
- There is no single technological solution that prevents phishing entirely.
- Reducing damage depends on layered defenses and strong awareness training.
- Security professionals often help others identify phishing and other social engineering by building training and promoting a culture of security.
