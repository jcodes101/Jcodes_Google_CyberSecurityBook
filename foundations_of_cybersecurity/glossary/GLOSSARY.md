# Glossary — Foundations of Cybersecurity (Modules 1–4)

Terms are listed **A–Z** by entry title. Some entries combine duplicate definitions from multiple modules.

---

### Advanced persistent threat (APT)

Threat actors with significant expertise at accessing a network without authorization; often research targets in advance and remain undetected for extended periods.

---

### Adversarial artificial intelligence (AI)

A technique that manipulates artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) technology to conduct attacks more efficiently.

---

### Antivirus software

A software program used to prevent, detect, and eliminate malware and viruses (also called anti-malware).

---

### Asset

An item perceived as having value to an organization.

---

### Automation

Using technology to reduce manual effort on repetitive tasks; lowers **human error** risk (common goal of security scripting).

---

### Availability

The idea that data is accessible to those who are authorized to access it. (Part of the CIA triad.)

---

### Business Email Compromise (BEC)

A type of phishing where a threat actor sends email that seems to be from a known source to make a seemingly legitimate request—often for information— to obtain a **financial advantage**.

---

### Center for Internet Security (CIS)

Nonprofit providing **controls** and practical guidance to harden systems and networks and to respond to incidents.

---

### Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP)

A globally recognized information security certification awarded by (ISC)².

---

### Chain of custody

Documented record of **who** possessed evidence, **when**, **where**, and **why** during an investigation or incident.

---

### Cloud security

The process of ensuring that assets stored in the cloud are properly configured and that access to those assets is limited to authorized users.

---

### Compliance

The process of adhering to internal standards and external regulations.

---

### Computer virus

Malicious code written to interfere with computer operations and cause damage to data and software. (Often shortened to **virus**.)

---

### Confidentiality

The idea that only authorized users can access specific assets or data. (Part of the CIA triad; in ethics, implies strong respect for privacy.)

---

### Confidentiality, integrity, availability (CIA) triad

A model that helps organizations consider risk when setting up systems and security policies.

---

### Cryptographic attack

An attack that affects secure forms of communication between a sender and an intended recipient (e.g., birthday, collision, downgrade attacks).

---

### Cybersecurity (or security)

The practice of ensuring **confidentiality, integrity, and availability** of information by protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or criminal exploitation.

---

### Data point

A specific piece of information (often within a large database).

---

### Database

An organized collection of information or data.

---

### FERC–NERC / Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP)

Regulatory context for organizations involved with **electricity** and the **North American power grid**; includes reliability and security reporting obligations.

---

### Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program (FedRAMP)

U.S. program standardizing **assessment, authorization, and monitoring** of **cloud services** for federal use.

---

### General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

EU regulation governing processing of EU residents’ data and privacy rights (including breach notification timelines).

---

### Hacker

Any person who uses computers to gain access to computer systems, networks, or data. (Motivation and authorization distinguish ethical vs. unethical use.)

---

### Hacktivist

A person who uses hacking to achieve a **political goal** (e.g., propaganda, social change, fame).

---

### Health Information Trust Alliance (HITRUST)

Security **framework and assurance** program that helps organizations align with **HIPAA** and related requirements.

---

### Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

A U.S. federal law that protects patients’ health information (PHI), governed by Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification rules.

---

### Integrity

The idea that data is correct, authentic, and reliable. (Part of the CIA triad.)

---

### Internal threat

A current or former employee, external vendor, or trusted partner who poses a security risk (accidental or intentional).

---

### International Organization for Standardization (ISO)

Body publishing international standards; security teams often encounter **management and security** standards (e.g., ISO/IEC 27001 family) in vendor and enterprise contexts.

---

### Intrusion detection system (IDS)

An application that monitors system activity and alerts on possible intrusions (e.g., by analyzing network packets).

---

### Linux

An open-source operating system (not a programming language); often used with a command-line interface.

---

### Log

A record of events that occur within an organization’s systems.

---

### Malware

Software designed to harm devices or networks.

---

### National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

U.S. agency developing voluntary guidance such as the **Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** and **Risk Management Framework (RMF)**.

---

### National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

A voluntary U.S. framework of standards, guidelines, and best practices to manage cybersecurity risk.

---

### Network security

The practice of keeping an organization’s network infrastructure secure from unauthorized access (data, services, systems, devices on the network).

---

### Open Web Application Security Project (OWASP)

Community resource; **OWASP Top 10** lists critical web application risks (review periodically).

---

### Order of volatility

Sequence for preserving digital evidence from **most volatile** (easily lost) to **more stable**—guides what to capture first.

---

### Password attack

An attempt to access password-secured devices, systems, networks, or data (e.g., brute force, rainbow table).

---

### Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)

International standard for secure handling of cardholder data (storage, processing, transmission).

---

### Penetration testing (pen testing)

**Authorized** simulated attack to find weaknesses in technology and **processes**.

---

### Personally identifiable information (PII)

Any information used to infer an individual’s identity.

---

### Phishing

The use of digital communications to trick people into revealing sensitive data or deploying malicious software.

---

### Physical attack

A security incident affecting digital **and** physical environments (e.g., malicious USB, card skimming).

---

### Physical social engineering

A threat actor impersonates an employee, customer, or vendor to obtain unauthorized access to a **physical location**.

---

### Playbook

Step-by-step **operational manual** (e.g., incident response procedure) analysts follow for consistent, policy-aligned work.

---

### Privacy protection

Safeguarding personal information from unauthorized use.

---

### Programming

A process used to create instructions for a computer to execute tasks (including security automation).

---

### Protected health information (PHI)

Information relating to past, present, or future physical or mental health, care, or payment—protected under HIPAA.

---

### Security architecture

Security design composed of multiple components (tools, processes) that protect an organization from risks and external threats.

---

### Security controls

Safeguards designed to reduce specific security risks; used with frameworks to build posture and meet compliance.

---

### Security ethics

Guidelines for making appropriate decisions as a security professional.

---

### Security frameworks

Guidelines for building plans that mitigate risks and threats to data and privacy.

---

### Security governance

Practices that support, define, and direct an organization’s security efforts.

---

### Security information and event management (SIEM)

An application that collects and analyzes log data to monitor critical activities and surface alerts.

---

### Security posture

An organization’s ability to manage defense of critical assets and data and react to change.

---

### Sensitive personally identifiable information (SPII)

A specific type of PII subject to stricter handling (e.g., SSN, payment card data).

---

### Smishing

Phishing via **SMS/text messages** to steal information or impersonate a trusted sender.

---

### Social engineering

A manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain private information, access, or valuables.

---

### Social media phishing

Collecting detailed target information from social media before initiating an attack.

---

### Spear phishing

Email attack targeting a specific user or group; appears to come from a trusted source.

---

### SQL (Structured Query Language)

A language used to create, interact with, and query databases.

---

### Supply-chain attack

Attack targeting systems, applications, hardware, and/or software to find a point to deploy malware—often via third parties in the supply chain.

---

### System and Organization Controls (SOC 1 / SOC 2)

AICPA-aligned **audit reports** on a service organization’s controls, often covering security, availability, confidentiality, privacy, and integrity.

---

### Technical skills

Skills requiring knowledge of specific tools, procedures, and policies.

---

### Threat

Any circumstance or event that can negatively impact assets.

---

### Threat actor

Any person or group who presents a security risk (malicious intent).

---

### Transferable skills

Skills from other domains that apply across careers.

---

### USB baiting

Leaving a malware-infected USB device for someone to find and plug in, infecting the network.

---

### Vishing

Voice-based phishing: exploiting phone/voice communication to obtain sensitive information or impersonate a trusted party.

---

### Watering hole attack

Compromising a website frequently visited by a specific group of users.

---

### Whaling

Spear phishing aimed at **executives** to access sensitive data.
