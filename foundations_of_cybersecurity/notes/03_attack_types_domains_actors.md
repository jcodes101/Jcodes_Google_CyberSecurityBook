# Attack types, CISSP domains, threat actors, and hackers

## Attack categories (with CISSP domain notes from course)

*Sections below are **A–Z** by attack category name.*

### Adversarial artificial intelligence

Manipulates **AI/ML** to make attacks faster or more effective.

**Domains:** Communication and network security **and** identity and access management.

---

### Cryptographic attack

Targets **secure communication** between sender and intended recipient.

**Examples:** birthday, collision, downgrade attacks.

**Domain:** Communication and network security.

---

### Password attack

Attempt to access **password-protected** devices, systems, networks, or data.

**Forms you will see later in the program:** brute force, rainbow table.

**Domain (course mapping):** Communication and network security.

---

### Physical attack

Incident affecting **digital and physical** layers—attack delivered via physical objects or presence.

**Examples:** malicious USB cable, malicious flash drive, card cloning/skimming.

**Domain:** Asset security.

---

### Social engineering attack

Manipulation exploiting **human error** for information, access, or valuables.

**Forms referenced in the program:** BEC, phishing, physical social engineering, smishing, social media phishing, spear phishing, USB baiting, vishing, watering hole, whaling.

**Domain:** Security and risk management.

---

### Supply-chain attack

Targets **systems, applications, hardware, or software** to find a weak point where **malware** can be inserted. Third parties in the **supply chain** mean many possible failure points; impact can spread across **many organizations**.

**Domains (examples):** Security and risk management; security architecture and engineering; security operations (among others).

---

## CISSP domains — key takeaway

The **eight CISSP security domains** help teams **prepare for and respond to** data breaches. Breaches vary from simple to complex and may span **more than one domain**. The attack types above are only a **sample**; the certificate program goes deeper over time.

## Threat actor types

*Subsections **A–Z** by actor label.*

### Advanced persistent threats (APTs)

- High skill at **unauthorized** network access.  
- Often **research targets** (large companies, governments) beforehand.  
- Can stay **hidden** for a long time.

**Possible motivations:**

- Damage **critical infrastructure** (e.g., power, natural resources).  
- Steal **intellectual property** (trade secrets, patents).

---

### Hacktivists

Motivated by **politics** or ideology; use digital tools for goals such as:

- Demonstrations, propaganda, social campaigns, **visibility/fame**.

---

### Insider threats

Abuse **legitimate access** to harm the organization.

**Possible motivations:** sabotage, corruption, espionage, unauthorized access or **data leaks**.

---

## Hacker categories (skills and authorization)

**Hacker (general):** Someone who uses computers to access systems, networks, or data—skill level and **intent** vary.

### Three main categories

| Category | Also called | Behavior |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Authorized** | Ethical hackers | Follow ethics and **law**; help organizations assess and reduce risk; protect against malicious actors. |
| **Semi-authorized** | Researchers | Find vulnerabilities but **do not exploit** them maliciously. |
| **Unauthorized** | Unethical / malicious hackers | Break or ignore laws; often steal/sell confidential data for **profit**. |

**Note:** Real-world labels overlap; one person may fit more than one category depending on context.

### Other patterns mentioned in the course

- **Mercenary / for-hire:** may do **legal or illegal** work depending on the job—ethical label depends on the work.  
- **New/unskilled actors:** learning, revenge, or using **existing tools/scripts** to exploit weaknesses.  
- **Vigilante hackers:** aim to stop unethical hackers (still operate outside normal law enforcement roles).

---

## Threat actor vs. hacker (distinction)

- **Threat actor** → defined by **malicious intent** and risk to the organization.  
- **Hacker** → defined by **technical access skills** and **motivations** (not always malicious).

Understanding **motivation** improves detection, prioritization, and communication with leadership.
