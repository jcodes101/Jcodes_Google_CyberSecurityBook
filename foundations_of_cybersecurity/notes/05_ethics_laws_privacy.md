# Ethics, laws, counterattacks, and privacy

## Counterattacks — United States

In the U.S., **striking back** at a threat actor is generally **illegal** under laws such as:

- **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986)**  
- **Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (2015)**  
- (Among others—course emphasizes “defend, don’t counterattack” for most practitioners.)

**Typical professional stance:** You **defend**; you do **not** launch retaliatory hacks.

### Why counterattack is discouraged

- Treated as **vigilantism** (non–law enforcement taking justice into their own hands).  
- Can **escalate** the incident and cause **more damage**.  
- If the attacker is **state-sponsored** or politically motivated, retaliation can have **serious international** consequences.

### Who may conduct authorized offensive operations

Generally **approved federal or military personnel**—not corporate security teams acting on their own.

## Counterattacks — international perspective (ICJ)

The **International Court of Justice** updates guidance over time. Course summary of **narrow** conditions under which a counterattack might be considered lawful in theory:

1. Effects **only** the party that attacked first.  
2. Is a **direct communication** demanding the attacker stop.  
3. Does **not escalate** the situation.  
4. Effects are **reversible**.

**Reality for organizations:** These tests are **hard to measure**; uncertainty and loss of control make **counterattack a poor business decision** for most teams—especially without deep legal and operational expertise.

**Further reading (course):** **Tallinn Manual** (online updates) for international cyber operations and legal framing.

## Ethics complements law

Because **counterattack** is usually illegal or disapproved, the field relies on **frameworks and controls** (e.g., **CIA triad**, standards from earlier notes) plus **professional ethics** to protect organizations and people.

---

## Confidentiality and privacy (professional ethics)

### Confidentiality

Only **authorized** users should access certain assets or data. Ethically: **high respect for privacy** when handling sensitive information.

### Privacy protection

Protecting **personal information** from unauthorized use.

- **PII** — can infer identity (e.g., name, phone).  
- **SPII** — stricter handling (e.g., SSN, payment card numbers).

**Ethical obligations** include securing private data, finding weaknesses, managing **organizational risk**, and **aligning security with business goals**.

---

## Laws — professional obligations

Laws are rules enforced by government. As a security professional you are expected to:

- Stay **unbiased**; work **honestly**, **responsibly**, and **within the law**.  
- Be **transparent** and **fair**; base decisions on **evidence**.  
- Stay **engaged** and **competent**; keep learning to handle issues ethically.  
- **Stay informed** to improve the broader security landscape.

### Example: HIPAA

HIPAA protects **PHI**. Organizations have **legal and ethical** duties—e.g., **informing patients** if healthcare data is exposed in a breach. Security staff often help **prove** and **support** compliant breach response.

---

## Key takeaway

**Ethics and law** shape daily decisions. When incidents occur, documented **policy** plus **legal/ethical** boundaries keep both the organization and **you** safer than improvised retaliation.
