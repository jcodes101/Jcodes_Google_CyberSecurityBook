# Post-incident activity (NIST Incident Response Lifecycle)

The **Post-incident activity** phase is the process of **reviewing** an incident to find **improvements** in how incidents are handled. The NIST lifecycle is **cyclical**—learning from one incident strengthens the next response.

---

## Lessons learned

After containment, eradication, and recovery, the immediate crisis may be over, but security work continues. Incidents are a chance to **learn** and **prioritize** improvements to incident handling.

### Lessons learned meeting (post-mortem)

A **lessons learned** meeting (sometimes called a **post-mortem**) brings together **everyone involved** after a **major** incident. Scope may require **more than one** session to collect enough information.

**Purpose:** Review the incident **end-to-end**, evaluate **response actions**, and identify **improvements**. The goal is **learning**, not **blame**.

**Timing:** Schedule **no later than two weeks** after the incident has been **successfully remediated** (per this course’s guidance).

**When needed:** Not every incident needs its own session—**size** and **severity** decide. **Major** incidents (e.g., **ransomware**) should get a dedicated review. Participants include anyone who took part in **any** part of the response.

### Example discussion questions

- What happened?  
- What time did it happen?  
- Who discovered it?  
- How was it contained?  
- What actions were taken for recovery?  
- What could have been done differently?  

**Additional benefit:** In large organizations, these meetings help **cross-functional** teams share information and **prevention** ideas.

**Pro tip:** Share an **agenda** in advance; assign roles such as **moderator** (facilitates discussion) and **scribe** (notes).

---

## Recommendations

Lessons learned should produce **prioritized**, **actionable** recommendations: fix errors in response, close **gaps** in processes or procedures, or strengthen **controls**. That way the organization does not repeat the same failure modes.

Examples of follow-through:

- Update **playbooks**  
- Deploy or tune **security tools**  

---

## Final report

Documentation across the lifecycle supports closing work. At minimum, incident records should address investigative basics—the **five W’s**: **who**, **what**, **where**, **why**, and **when**.

A **final report** gives a **comprehensive** review of the incident. Formats are **not** universal; organizations (and audiences) differ. **Multiple** reports may exist for different readers.

### Common elements

**Executive summary**  
High-level overview: **key findings** and **essential facts**.

**Timeline**  
Chronological sequence with **timestamps** for events leading to and through the incident.

**Investigation**  
Actions taken during **detection and analysis**—for example, review of a **network artifact** such as a **packet capture** to explain what occurred on the network.

**Recommendations**  
Suggested actions to **prevent** recurrence or improve readiness.

**Pro tip:** Write for the **audience**. Executives and other non-security readers may need **clear**, **minimal jargon** explanations of what mattered and what happens next.

---

## Key takeaways

- **Post-incident activity** closes the loop: **review** the response, **capture** lessons, **assign** improvements, and **document** outcomes.  
- **Lessons learned** meetings focus on improvement, not blame, and should yield **prioritized recommendations**.  
- The **final report** consolidates the story (often including timeline, investigation, and recommendations) for operational and leadership audiences.
