# Security audits and audit checklists

## What is a security audit?

A **security audit** is a **review** of an organization’s **security controls**, **policies**, and **procedures** against a defined set of **expectations**.

- Audits are **independent** evaluations of whether the organization **meets** criteria.  
- **Internal criteria:** policies, procedures, **best practices** the org adopted.  
- **External criteria:** **regulations**, **laws**, **federal** requirements.  

Audits also assess whether **established controls** actually run as intended (**security controls** = safeguards that reduce **specific** risks).

### Why audits matter

- Confirm **checks** happen (e.g., **daily** review of **SIEM** dashboards) to surface **threats**, **risks**, and **vulnerabilities**.  
- Support **security posture**.  
- When issues appear, a **remediation** path must exist.

---

## Goals and objectives

| | |
|--|--|
| **Goal** | Ensure **IT practices** meet **industry** and **organizational** standards. |
| **Objective** | Find **gaps** (remediation and improvement), **clarify** what is failing, and **plan** fixes. |

Audits help **protect data** and **avoid** fines and penalties from regulators. **How often** you audit depends on **local law**, **federal** rules, and **contract** terms.

---

## Factors that shape audit programs

What gets audited and how formal it is often depends on:

- **Industry** (healthcare, finance, etc.)  
- **Organization size**  
- **Applicable government** regulations  
- **Geography** (jurisdiction)  
- **Voluntary** commitment to a **compliance** regime  

For a compact map of **regulations** and **frameworks**, see Course 1 notes: [../../foundations_of_cybersecurity/notes/04_frameworks_compliance_standards.md](../../foundations_of_cybersecurity/notes/04_frameworks_compliance_standards.md).

## Frameworks, controls, and audits

Frameworks help organizations **prepare** for compliance-driven audits:

- **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF)**  
- **ISO/IEC 27000** series (information security)

Using **frameworks** with **controls** speeds **internal** and **external** audits and aligns evidence with **requirements**.

During audits, reviewers typically examine **three control categories**:

1. **Administrative / managerial**  
2. **Technical**  
3. **Physical**  

Detailed examples and **control types** (preventative, detective, etc.): [07_control_categories_matrix.md](07_control_categories_matrix.md).

## Audit checklist (typical structure)

Build a **checklist** **before** the audit. Common sections:

1. **Define scope**  
   - **Assets** in scope (e.g., firewalls configured correctly, **PII** protected, **physical** assets secured).  
   - How the audit **supports** organizational **goals**.  
   - **Cadence** (how often the audit runs).  
   - Whether **policies** and **procedures** are **documented**, **current**, and **followed** by staff.  

2. **Risk assessment**  
   - Evaluate risks tied to **budget**, **controls**, **internal** processes, and **external** standards (**regulations**).  

3. **Conduct the audit**  
   - For **internal** audits, assess **security** of scoped **assets** against criteria.  

4. **Mitigation plan**  
   - Plan to **lower** risk and **costs** (fines, downtime, reputation) from findings.  

5. **Report to stakeholders**  
   - Deliver **findings**, **recommended** improvements, and **mapping** to **compliance** obligations.
