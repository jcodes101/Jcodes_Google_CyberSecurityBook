# Who are stakeholders?

A **stakeholder** is an **individual or group** with an **interest** in an organization’s decisions or activities. As a **security analyst**, much of your work includes **reporting findings** to different **security stakeholders**.

Organizations vary; titles and reporting lines differ. The roles below are **common examples** of who may care about security outcomes.

---

## Levels of stakeholders

You may only talk to a **subset** of stakeholders directly, especially early in your career. It still helps to know **who** exists at higher levels and **what** they optimize for.

### Cybersecurity risk manager

Leads efforts to **identify**, **assess**, and **mitigate** security **risks** across the organization.

### Chief Executive Officer (CEO)

The **highest-ranking** leader in the organization. Entry-level analysts **rarely** interact with the CEO directly on security matters.

### Chief Financial Officer (CFO)

A senior leader focused on **financial** health and risk. Entry-level analysts **rarely** engage the CFO directly.

### Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)

The senior-most **security** leadership role in many enterprises. Entry-level analysts **rarely** meet with the CISO day to day.

### Operations manager (security operations)

Oversees **day-to-day** security operations: teams that **develop** and **run** security practices that protect the organization from threats.

### How priorities differ at a glance

**CFOs** and **CISOs** often focus on **big-picture** risk—financial exposure, regulatory and strategic security posture—while **operations managers** focus on **daily** impact: outages, workload, queue health, and tactical response.

Even if you do not email the CISO weekly, their decisions (budget, policy, risk appetite) shape the tools and processes you use.

---

## Stakeholder communications for entry-level analysts

Two stakeholders you may work with **often** are **operations managers** and **risk managers**.

When you report upward, be **clear** about:

- the **current** security issue  
- plausible **causes** (what you know vs what you suspect)  

The **operations manager** often decides **next steps** and coordinates remediation—for example, validating whether activity is benign or malicious and managing operational side effects.

### Example: failed login attempts

You might report **repeated failed logins** for an employee account. The operations manager might loop in the employee’s **supervisor** to confirm mistyped passwords versus **account compromise**. They may also weigh **business impact** (for example, **lockouts** that block legitimate work).

After leadership agrees on a path, you might help implement **preventive** controls (detection tuning, awareness reminders, MFA checks—whatever the organization standardizes).

---

## From one stakeholder to the next

**Operations managers** and **risk managers** depend on analysts and other staff to surface **what is happening** in daily operations. They typically **summarize** status, trends, and major incidents **upward** to **CISOs** and **CFOs** so senior leaders see the **overall** security story.

Your detailed tickets and reports become **inputs** to those summaries—even when you never present directly to the C-suite.

---

## Key takeaways

- **Stakeholders** care about security outcomes; analysts **communicate** upward with facts and context.  
- Know the **difference** between **strategic** leaders (CEO/CFO/CISO) and **operational** leaders (operations managers, risk managers).  
- Entry-level analysts usually **pair** with **operations** and **risk** roles first; that work still **feeds** reporting to the **highest** levels of the organization.
