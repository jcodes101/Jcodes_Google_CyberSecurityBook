# Incident escalation: data-related roles

Organizations differ: **role names**, **reporting lines**, and **definitions** are not universal. The labels below describe common patterns you may see when incidents are **escalated** and when **data** responsibilities are involved.

---

## Data owners

A **data owner** decides who may **access**, **edit**, **use**, or **destroy** their information. Owners have **administrative control** over specific information, hardware, or software and are **accountable** for **classification**, **protection**, **access**, and **use** of that data.

**Example escalation:** An employee gains **unauthorized access** to software they do **not** need for work—this may be escalated to the **data owner** of that software.

---

## Data controllers

**Data controllers** define **why** and **how** personal data is **processed** (procedure and purpose). The role often centers on **customers’** personal information: the controller decides **how** that data is used and works to ensure use, storage, and processing meet **security** and **privacy** rules.

**Example escalation:** **Sensitive customer information** is at risk—often escalated to **data controllers**.

---

## Data processors

**Data processors** act **on behalf of** the data controller and **carry out** processing. They commonly report to the controller and are often **vendors**. Processors may implement **technical** and **organizational** measures to protect data.

**Example escalation:** Processing failures or vendor-side issues are often escalated to whoever **oversees** the **third party** responsible for processing.

---

## Data custodians

**Data custodians** **grant** and **revoke** access to systems or data, implement **security controls** for the data they steward, shape **how** data is **stored** and **transmitted**, advise on **threats**, and **monitor** the data. They are typically involved when controls must be **strengthened** or when controls may have been **compromised**.

---

## Data protection officers (DPOs)

**Data protection officers (DPOs)** monitor **internal compliance** with the organization’s **data protection** procedures. They advise security and the business on **obligations** under applicable standards, and may **assess** whether measures **adequately** protect data.

**Example escalation:** When **standards** or **protocols** appear **violated**, DPOs are often notified.

---

## Key takeaways

- Escalation works best when teams act **coherently**; know who owns **decisions** vs **operations** vs **compliance**.  
- **Entry-level** analysts usually escalate through their **direct supervisor** first—but learning these roles early helps you route **data** and **privacy** incidents correctly as you grow.  
- Always map these concepts to **your organization’s** org chart and policy names.
