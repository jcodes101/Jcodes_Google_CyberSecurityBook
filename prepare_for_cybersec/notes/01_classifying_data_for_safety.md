# Classifying for safety

Security professionals **classify data** so they can **protect** the organization from attacks that disrupt business. Common classification levels include:

- **Public** data  
- **Private** data  
- **Sensitive** data  
- **Confidential** data  

Each organization defines these categories in its own **data classification policy**—you should learn and follow **your employer’s** policy.

---

## Public data

**Public data** is already available to the public and carries **minimal** organizational risk if viewed or shared. It still needs **basic** security controls (for example integrity and availability protections) because it can still be abused or defaced.

**Examples:** press releases, job descriptions, marketing materials.

---

## Private data

**Private data** has a **higher** protection bar than public data. It is information that **should not** be exposed broadly. **Unauthorized** access can pose a **serious** risk.

**Examples:** company email addresses, employee identification numbers, organizational research data.

---

## Sensitive data

**Sensitive data** must be protected from anyone **without** authorization. Unauthorized access can **seriously** harm finances and **reputation**.

This category often includes:

- **Personally identifiable information (PII)**  
- **Sensitive personally identifiable information (SPII)**  
- **Protected health information (PHI)**  

**Examples:** bank account numbers, usernames and passwords, Social Security numbers (U.S. wage reporting identifier), passport numbers, medical information.

---

## Confidential data

**Confidential data** is critical to **ongoing** business operations. Access is often **limited** to a small set of people and may require **non-disclosure agreements (NDAs)**—legal contracts that bind parties to protect shared information.

**Examples:** proprietary information such as **trade secrets**, financial records, and sensitive government-related data.

---

## Asset classification

**Asset classification** means **labeling assets** by **sensitivity** and **importance** to the organization. Levels typically range from **low** to **high**.

- **Public** data is generally a **low-level** asset: widely available; limited downside if integrity/availability issues are handled.  
- **Sensitive** and **confidential** data are **high-level** assets: disclosure can damage **competitive position**, **reputation**, and **customer trust**.

**Illustrative contrast:** A company’s **public website URL** may be a **low-level** asset. An **internal email** discussing **trade secrets** is a **high-level** asset.

---

## Key takeaways

- Organizations use **data classification policies** to say what belongs in each category—**read and apply** yours at work.  
- Understanding **public / private / sensitive / confidential** helps you **prioritize** controls and incident response.  
- **Asset classification** ties labels to **business impact**, so you know what deserves **stronger** safeguards versus **baseline** protection.
