# Communicating with stakeholders: get to the point

Security stakeholders have **time-sensitive** responsibilities that **affect the business**. Messages to them—and the **actions** you need—should be **clear** and **direct**.

---

## Get to the point

Before you write or speak, ask:

1. **What** do I want this person to know?  
2. **Why** is it important for them to know it?  
3. **When** do they need to take action?  
4. **How** can I explain the situation in a **non-technical** way (when the audience is not deeply technical)?  

Leading with **outcomes**, **risk**, and **required decisions** reduces friction and speeds response.

---

## Follow the protocols

When you join a team, learn **how** the organization expects you to communicate: **approved tools**, **tickets**, **escalation paths**, and **record-keeping** rules.

Confirm what is acceptable before you reach stakeholders—for example:

- in-person meetings  
- video conferencing  
- email  
- company chat or collaboration apps  

Following protocol protects **privacy**, **evidence integrity**, and **auditability**.

---

## Communicate with impact

You already mapped **who** stakeholders are (see note **03**). Early in your career you often work with **operational** stakeholders (for example **operations managers** or **security risk managers**) who care about **day-to-day** signals—queues, alerts, **logging**, and service impact.

**Senior** stakeholders may care more about **aggregate risk** and **business** consequences (for example **financial** exposure from an incident) than raw log lines.

### Tailor content to the role

When reporting to an **operations manager**, emphasize what matters to **daily operations**: unusual patterns you are escalating, affected systems, severity, and what you need from them **now**.

Matching the message to their **responsibilities** keeps updates **need-to-know** and actionable.

---

## Communication methods

Pick a channel that fits the **message**:

- **Instant messaging** — quick questions, short updates, fast coordination  
- **Email** — documented detail, multiple recipients, attachments  
- **Video calling** — complex topics, alignment meetings, screen sharing  
- **Phone calls** — urgent clarifications when chat is too slow  
- **Spreadsheets** — structured numeric or tabular evidence  
- **Slides** — executive summaries, trends, training, or incident briefings  

**Heuristics:**

- Straightforward updates → chat or a quick call  
- Layered, nuanced situations → email or a scheduled meeting  
- Lots of metrics or comparisons → charts, dashboards, or a spreadsheet  

The **medium** should reinforce clarity, not replace it.

---

## Key takeaways

- Stakeholders are **busy** and care about **specific** slices of risk and operations—**filter** to what matters to **their** role.  
- Learn and follow **organizational communication protocols**.  
- Match **depth**, **tone**, and **channel** to the audience and the **type** of information you are sharing.
